Why is the sky blue?
The sky appears blue due to a phenomenon called Rayleigh scattering. Here's a brief explanation:

1. **Sunlight Composition**: Sunlight, or white light, is composed of light of different colors, which have different wavelengths. 
2. **Earth's Atmosphere**: When sunlight passes through the Earth's atmosphere, it interacts with air molecules, water droplets, and dust particles.
3. **Scattering of Light**: Light with shorter wavelengths (blue and violet) is scattered more than light with longer wavelengths (red and yellow) due to the smaller size of the gas molecules relative to the wavelength of the incident light.
4. **Human Vision**: The human eye is more sensitive to blue light than violet light, and the upper atmosphere also absorbs violet light, so the sky appears blue to us during the day.
5. **Angle of Sunlight**: At sunrise and sunset, sunlight passes through a thicker layer of the atmosphere, which scatters shorter wavelength light out of the direct path of sight and allows longer wavelength light (reds and oranges) to reach the observer's eyes.

Rayleigh scattering is more effective at shorter wavelengths, hence blue light is scattered more strongly, and when we look at the sky away from the sun, the scattered blue is what dominates our perception.

Why is the sky blue?
The sky appears blue due to a phenomenon called Rayleigh scattering. Solar light that reaches Earth's atmosphere is made up of different colors that correspond to different wavelengths. When this light encounters the gas molecules and particles in the Earth's atmosphere, it is scattered in all directions. 

Rayleigh scattering occurs more effectively at shorter wavelengths, which are at the blue end of the visible light spectrum. Thus, when sunlight passes through the atmosphere, the short-wavelength blue light is scattered more than the other colors, making the sky appear predominantly blue to an observer on the ground during the daytime.

At sunset and sunrise, the sky can appear red and orange because the light travels through a greater depth of the atmosphere, scattering away the blue light and allowing the longer wavelengths, such as red and orange, to become more visible.

The sky appears blue to the human eye because of the phenomenon known as Rayleigh scattering. Here's the concise explanation:

1. **Sunlight Composition**: Sunlight contains all colors of the visible spectrum. Colors range from violet, with the shortest wavelength, to red, with the longest wavelength.

2. **Atmospheric Interaction**: As sunlight enters Earth's atmosphere, it interacts with air molecules, water droplets, and dust particles.

3. **Rayleigh Scattering**: Smaller atmospheric particles scatter shorter wavelengths of light (blue and violet) more than longer wavelengths (red and yellow).

4. **Human Vision and Sky Color**: Our eyes are more sensitive to blue than violet, and the upper atmosphere absorbs violet light. Additionally, the sun's light comes from a particular direction, causing the scattered blue light to dominate the sky's appearance.

5. **Viewing Angle**: At sunrise and sunset, the sunlight path through the atmosphere is longer, so even more of the blue light is scattered out, allowing reds and oranges to become more visible, which is why the sky can change color at different times of the day. 

Hence, due to selective scattering of sunlight by the Earth's atmosphere, the sky is primarily blue during the day.

Why is the sky blue?
The sky appears blue to the human eye because of the way Earth's atmosphere scatters sunlight. Sunlight is made up of a spectrum of colors that, when combined, appear white to us.

This scattering is caused by a phenomenon known as Rayleigh scattering. Here is what occurs:

1. **Atmospheric Composition**: Earth's atmosphere is filled with gases and particles that are smaller than the wavelengths of visible light.
2. **Scattering of Sunlight**: As sunlight enters the atmosphere, it collides with these gas molecules and particles.
3. **Wavelength Dependency**: Shorter wavelengths of light (blue and violet) are scattered more than longer wavelengths (red, orange, yellow, green).
4. **Human Vision Sensitivity**: Human eyes are more sensitive to blue light than violet light, and furthermore, some of the violet light gets absorbed by the upper atmosphere, which is why the sky does not appear violet even though violet light is scattered even more than blue light.
5. **Direct Path Less Scattered**: As for the longer wavelengths of light, such as red and orange, these are scattered less and tend to continue in a straighter path, which is why the sun and sky can appear red at sunrise and sunset when the sun's light has to pass through more atmosphere.

Hence, the dominant scattered color in the sky we observe is blue, which is why we perceive the sky as being that color during daylight hours.

2. **Scattering**: Similar to the sky, water molecules can scatter sunlight. This scattering favors shorter wavelengths of light, which are blue and violet. The blue light is scattered in all directions and some of it reaches our eyes, contributing to the perception of blue water.

The sky appears blue due to a phenomenon called Rayleigh scattering. The Earth's atmosphere is composed of various gases and particles that are smaller than the wavelength of visible light. When sunlight enters the atmosphere, the shorter (blue) wavelengths of light are scattered in all directions by these gas molecules and particles more than the other colors (longer wavelengths) because shorter wavelengths scatter more efficiently.

This scattering causes the blue color to become more pronounced as it reaches our eyes from all directions of the sky. Though this scattering also affects other colors, blue, being at the shorter wavelength end of the visible spectrum, is scattered more and is therefore more visible.

Additionally, our eyes are more sensitive to blue light. The combination of more efficient scattering of blue light and the physiology of human vision results in the sky appearing predominantly blue during the day.

1. Cohesion and Adhesion: Water molecules are cohesive, meaning they stick to each other due to hydrogen bonding. They also demonstrate adhesion, sticking to other substances. This combination allows water to wet surfaces by spreading over them and adhering to them.

2. Sensation on Skin: When water comes in contact with our skin, it disrupts the equilibrium of the skin's surface, creating a sensation that our nerves interpret as wetness. Our skin can detect this change because water cools the skin as it evaporates, which is sensed by our nerves.

3. Evaporation: Water can absorb a significant amount of heat energy as it evaporates, which cools the surface it's on. This cooling effect is also part of the sensation of wetness.

4. Physical Properties: Water’s fluidity and low viscosity allow it to fill the pores and irregularities of most surfaces, providing the sensation of wetness when touched.

"Wetness" is actually a description of our perception of water or other liquids, rather than an inherent property of the liquid itself. In other words, water doesn't contain wetness; it causes the feeling we know as being wet.
